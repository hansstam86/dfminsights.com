Why DFM Matters

The five mistakes that kill hardware programs

These aren't edge cases. They happen on almost every first-time hardware program — and every one of them is detectable weeks before it becomes expensive.

1
The tolerance stack-up that shows up at DVT
Individual component dimensions are each within spec. But when assembled, the cumulative worst-case variation causes the lid to rattle, the gasket to not seal, or the PCB to bind. A worst-case stack-up analysis at EVT takes 30 minutes. The tooling change to fix it at DVT takes 3 weeks.
2
The antenna keep-out that was moved "just once"
The Qi charging coil got repositioned to fix the centre-of-gravity balance. Nobody updated the BT antenna keep-out. Range drops 10%. A 3D EM simulation would have flagged the coupling in an afternoon. Discovering it in EVT range testing means a PCB respin.
3
The component substitution nobody documented
During a shortage, the CM substituted a 60A Shore gasket for the specified 70A. It passed EVT. It failed IP54 at DVT. The substitution was never captured in the BOM. A receiving inspection protocol and a "no substitute" flag on the BOM line would have prevented it.
4
The EMC failure that could have been a $0.45 fix
The DC-DC converter switching frequency harmonics fail FCC Part 15B at DVT. A ferrite clamp on the power cable — $0.45 — would have resolved it. But it needs to be in the DVT-2 BOM, which means it needs to be identified before DVT-1, which means it needs an EMC pre-scan at EVT. Which nobody scheduled.
5
The biocompatibility failure at DVT on a $0.40 pigment
The red silicone strap looked great in the CAD. At DVT, the ISO 10993 cytotoxicity test flags the red pigment at >0.5% concentration. The medical-grade certified alternative costs $0.40 more per unit. The 8-week lab re-test and DVT delay costs $40,000.
